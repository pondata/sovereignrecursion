The problem

Every knowledge-driven organization has the same accumulating crisis. Decades of email, conversation transcripts, partner notes, deal histories, technical decisions, client memos, and internal documents sit across dozens of systems, and not one of them is searchable in a way that returns useful results. Senior people retire and their accumulated judgment leaves the building. The CIO is asked at every board meeting "what's our AI strategy" and there is no good answer because the layer underneath — structured access to the firm's institutional knowledge — does not exist.

Most "AI strategy" pitches landing in your inbox right now are slideware. Vendors are selling the future tense. What's missing is anyone showing you a working reference implementation and saying "here's an architecture that already works, here's what it would look like applied to your domain, here's the cost and timeline."

The reference implementation

A working personal AI knowledge graph called Codex Vivus, built over the last eighteen months. It indexes 4,948 conversations totaling 37.7 million words. Local-first (SQLite + FTS5). Full-text search returning ranked results in under 100 ms across the entire corpus. Tier-scoring. Topic clustering. Lint analysis for unresolved threads. A writeback layer that generates topic summaries.

It runs on a laptop. Has been running for over a year. The detailed case study, including architecture and lessons, is here: Codex Vivus: a working reference architecture for organizational AI knowledge.

Codex Vivus is personal infrastructure, not a product. The architectural pattern it embodies is what's offered as a service.

Engagement patterns

Pattern A — The departing-partner archive

Mid-size law firms and professional-services firms where senior partners are within five years of retirement. Their accumulated client knowledge, judgment, and unresolved questions live in their email and their head. The deliverable: a knowledge graph that ingests the partner's archive (with consent and matter-level scoping), structured exit interviews, and engagement memos — producing a queryable institutional artifact that survives the partner's departure.

$75k–$150k · 8–12 weeks

Pattern B — The deal-history graph

Family offices and venture firms with 10–30 years of deal flow, partner introductions, due-diligence memos, and portfolio-company notes. The deliverable: an indexed graph that can answer "have we ever looked at a company like this before", "who introduced us to X", and "what did our analysis say about industry Y in 2017".

$100k–$200k · 12–16 weeks

Pattern C — The technical archive

Architecture/engineering firms, defense contractors, and research-driven organizations with decades of technical documents, project artifacts, and design rationale. The deliverable: a structured graph layered onto the existing document store, with retention rules, classification handling, and integration with the firm's existing security perimeter.

$100k–$300k · 12–16 weeks

Pattern D — The fractional knowledge officer

Smaller engagement: an organization that's been pitched AI strategy a hundred times and wants someone with a working reference architecture to spend two days a month with their leadership team — diagnosing where institutional knowledge is leaking, prototyping fixes, and assessing AI-vendor pitches.

$5k–$10k/month · ongoing

How engagements work

Fixed-scope, fixed-price. No hourly billing for build work. The deliverable is a working system, not a deck. Remote by default with one or two in-person workshops during the engagement. The codebase and the data are yours. The schema and methodology are documented. The system is maintainable by your internal team after delivery.

Local-first architecture: your data stays on your hardware, the schema is yours, no cloud dependencies for core capability. SaaS vendors and LLM APIs become tools called by the system, not platforms it depends on.
